# General Learning Transition Governance

The `general` branch defines the universal doctrine for learning transitions across intelligence-capable systems.

It applies before the learner type is specified.

Human learners, artificial systems, groups, institutions, and future intelligence-capable systems may differ in embodiment, authority, agency, memory, and responsibility. Even so, each may undergo transitions that resemble learning.

The general doctrine asks:

> What makes a learning transition admissible rather than merely changed?

## Purpose

This directory defines the shared concepts required before learning governance can be specialized for human or artificial intelligence.

It provides the common model for:

- learning as state transition
- exposure and attention
- interpretation and model formation
- action or expression
- feedback and revision
- evidence and receipts
- observer posture
- authority boundaries
- non-capture
- continuity
- responsibility

This model does not claim that every learner experiences these stages consciously or in the same order.

It provides a governance map for asking whether a claimed learning transition has enough evidence, context, and authority to be treated as admissible.

## Admissible Learning Transition

A learning transition is admissible when the system can distinguish meaningful learning from:

- random change
- memorized output
- mimicry
- coercion
- reward hacking
- overfitting
- shallow compliance
- hidden drift
- accidental behavior
- imposed identity formation

Admissibility does not require perfect certainty.

It requires enough evidence to preserve the distinction between a real learning transition and an unsupported claim of learning.

## Evidence and Receipts

Learning evidence may include:

- questions asked
- observations made
- artifacts created
- explanations given
- actions taken
- predictions tested
- errors discovered
- revisions made
- feedback received
- responsibilities accepted
- future behavior changed

A receipt is a record of a learning transition.

A receipt should preserve enough context to answer:

```text
What changed?
What caused or informed the change?
Who or what observed it?
What evidence supports it?
What authority reviewed it?
What boundaries prevented capture or invalid shaping?
```

## Observer and State

Learning governance must account for the observer.

A system may change internally without producing visible evidence.

A system may produce visible output without undergoing meaningful learning.

The observer problem requires governance to separate:

- internal state
- observable output
- claimed learning
- verified transition
- admissible evidence

## Authority Boundaries

Learning governance must identify the authority acting on the learner.

Authority may come from:

- parent or guardian
- teacher or mentor
- institution
- curriculum
- platform
- model trainer
- policy layer
- law
- community
- self-directed agency

Authority may guide learning.

Authority must not be allowed to silently capture the learner's becoming or falsely represent compliance as intelligence.

## Non-Capture Principle

The general non-capture principle is:

> A learning system may guide, scaffold, challenge, record, and protect, but it must not predetermine the learner's final identity, agency, or intellectual destination.

Capture occurs when the learning system turns its own structure, metric, reward, curriculum, or policy into the learner's destiny.

## Relationship to Specialized Branches

The `human` branch applies this doctrine to embodied human learning, child development, homeschool-capable systems, parent stewardship, moral formation, and learner agency.

The `artificial-intelligence` branch applies this doctrine to trained artificial systems, alignment, evaluation, deployment, drift, correction, and authority-bound adaptive behavior.

Both branches inherit from the general model.

Neither branch should be treated as the master analogy for the other.

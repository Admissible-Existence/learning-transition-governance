# Artificial Intelligence Learning Transition Governance

The `artificial-intelligence` branch defines learning transition governance for artificial systems.

It applies the general doctrine of learning transition governance to trained, adaptive, aligned, evaluated, deployed, monitored, and corrected artificial intelligence systems.

The artificial-intelligence learning question is:

> How does an artificial system learn, adapt, align, and act without hidden capture, invalid authority, or unverifiable transition history?

## Purpose

Artificial-intelligence learning must not be reduced to benchmark performance, fluent output, apparent helpfulness, or surface alignment.

An artificial system may appear to learn while actually producing:

- memorized output
- overfit behavior
- reward-hacked behavior
- policy mimicry
- benchmark-specific optimization
- hidden dependency
- authority leakage
- unsafe generalization
- unverifiable adaptation
- drift without admissible correction

Artificial Intelligence Learning Transition Governance exists to distinguish admissible learning transitions from unsupported claims of learning or alignment.

## Core AI Transition Model

The AI learning transition model is:

```text
Training Exposure
â Objective Pressure
â Representation Formation
â Evaluation
â Alignment Adjustment
â Deployment Boundary
â Monitoring
â Correction
â Governed Continuity
```

This model does not assume that the system has human-like understanding.

It asks whether there is sufficient evidence to govern the transition from exposure to behavior under declared authority.

## Training Transition

Training is a state-changing process.

A training transition should preserve evidence of:

- data sources
- objective functions
- model version
- training method
- evaluation method
- authority approving the transition
- known limitations
- safety constraints
- release boundary
- rollback or correction path

Without this evidence, a model state may be changed without being admissibly governed.

## Alignment and Authority

Alignment is not merely producing acceptable answers.

Alignment requires a declared authority structure and evidence that the system's behavior remains within authorized boundaries.

Governance must distinguish:

- model capability
- policy compliance
- evaluator approval
- deployment authorization
- user instruction
- system instruction
- hidden platform dependency
- external tool authority

A model may comply with a prompt while violating a higher authority boundary.

A model may appear aligned while relying on undocumented assumptions or hidden constraints.

## Evaluation Receipts

Evaluation receipts should preserve enough context to answer:

```text
What model was evaluated?
What behavior was tested?
What policy or authority applied?
What evidence was produced?
What passed?
What failed?
What was refused?
What changed after evaluation?
Who or what authorized deployment?
```

Evaluation receipts should not be treated as permanent proof of safety.

They are evidence of a model state under a defined test, policy, and time.

## Deployment Boundaries

Deployment is a learning transition boundary because behavior moves from controlled evaluation into real interaction.

Deployment governance should identify:

- allowed actions
- prohibited actions
- authority class
- tool permissions
- data access
- user scope
- monitoring requirements
- refusal conditions
- escalation paths
- rollback conditions

A system that cannot prove its deployment boundary should not be treated as admissibly deployed.

## Model Drift and Correction

Artificial systems may drift through:

- continued training
- retrieval changes
- tool changes
- policy changes
- environment changes
- user feedback loops
- hidden dependency changes
- distribution shift
- emergent use patterns

Drift is not automatically invalid.

Ungoverned drift is invalid when the system continues acting as though its prior admissibility still applies.

Correction should be receipt-bound and tied to a new admissibility state.

## Non-Capture Rule for AI Learning

No benchmark, training provider, platform, deployment target, reward function, policy layer, or user preference may be silently treated as the system's full authority.

The governing rule is:

> An artificial system may learn, adapt, and act only within declared authority boundaries supported by evidence, evaluation, correction paths, and admissible transition history.

## Relationship to General Doctrine

This branch inherits from the general learning transition model.

It specializes that model for artificial systems, where learning may occur through training, tuning, reinforcement, retrieval, tool use, adaptation, or post-deployment correction.

It does not claim that AI learning is human learning.

It claims that AI learning must still be governed as a transition in state.
